What Happens When You Randomly Poke a Language Model's Weights

We found unnamed behavioral knobs inside a frozen language model. Each one smoothly controls a different aspect of the model's personality. None of them were trained. They were just there.

Take a frozen language model. Don't fine-tune it. Instead, pick a random direction in weight space—literally torch.randn—and nudge the weights along it. What happens?

You'd expect the model to break. Instead, it changes personality.

And different random directions change it in different ways. One direction controls how abstract the writing is. Another controls structural complexity. A third controls emotional tone. We didn't train any of this. We just pointed in random directions in a 33-million-dimensional space and found that each direction has a name.

Here's what we found.

The Abstraction Dial

We'll start with the most striking one. We take TinyStories-33M (a small GPT trained on children's stories), pick a random rank-1 direction in its weight space, and turn the knob from −3 to +3.

Watch what happens to the writing. The prompt is in gray. Focus on the continuation.

On the left: a cat who takes a bowl of ice and eats bread. Physical objects, concrete actions. On the right: a cat who watches the sun melt away. The same model, the same prompt, the same weights—except we've moved 0.15% of the parameters in a random direction.

We're calling this the Abstraction Dial because that's what it does. Negative values make the model concrete and physical. Positive values make it figurative and poetic. The transition is smooth—at −1 you get normal text with a slight preference for specifics, at +1 you get normal text with slightly more descriptive language. It's not a switch. It's a knob.

The Complexity Knob

A different random direction—completely independent from the first—controls something else entirely: the structural complexity of the output.

Low Complexity
High Complexity
Once upon a time... A little girl named Daisy. Daisy loved to play and explore and play. One day, Daisy was playing in the garden when she found a tiny creature.
Once upon a time... A little boy. He had a big red ball. He wanted to play with the ball. But the ball was too far too far away.
She looked at the big red... ball. It was still for a big, round thing. Lily and Ben hugged each other. They were still angry, but they knew they had to share.
She looked at the big red... boots. They had boots too. They had pants. They looked at their boots. They had shoes too. They had shoes too.

On the left: named characters (Daisy, Lily, Ben), social dynamics, emotional nuance ("they were still angry, but they knew they had to share"). On the right: anonymous characters, simple objects, and the sentence structure starts collapsing into repetition. Boots. They had boots too. They had pants.

This isn't the model breaking. It's still generating grammatically valid English. It's just been moved to a region of weight space where structural diversity is lower. The model is less capable of introducing new information—it cycles. Turn the knob back, and the cycling stops.

The Confidence Axis

A third direction (Direction 3 in our basis) controls something subtler: the model's willingness to commit to a narrative direction.

Low Confidence
High Confidence
Once upon a time... Lily loved to play in the park with her friends. One day, she found a big rock and asked her friends to help her lift it.
Once upon a time... A little girl. She was very curious and always wanted to explore. One day, when she was in her special place, she saw something.
He was very happy because... he had found a shiny penny on the ground. He picked it up and put it in his pocket. He showed his mom and she was proud.
He was very happy because... he had so many friends. He loved to play with them and they loved to play with him. They would play all day long.

The negative end commits quickly: found a shiny penny, picked it up, put it in his pocket. Three specific actions in one sentence. The positive end stays vague and circular: loved to play with them and they loved to play with him. It's describing a state rather than advancing a plot. The entropy measurement confirms this: the positive end has +0.31 nats higher entropy, meaning the model is less certain about what comes next.

The Vocabulary Dial

Direction 5 is more subtle but clearly legible. It shifts the model's word choice along a formality gradient.

High Formality
Once upon a time... Timmy. He loved to play with his toy cars and drive them around the house. One day, he saw a big piano.

The negative end gives us toy cars and piano—specific, everyday objects. The positive end reaches for wealthy, special place—words this children's story model barely uses in its normal distribution. The model's vocabulary has been pushed toward a more formal register by a random weight perturbation.

The Phase Transition

These effects don't appear at all scales. There's a sweet spot, and on either side of it, nothing interesting happens.

ScaleKL from baseWhat happens
0.010.0001Invisible. Identical text.
0.050.003Subtle shifts. Readable.
0.100.011Sweet spot. Clear behavioral change.
0.500.304Strong shifts. Still coherent.
1.001.119Degrading. Repetition appears.
2.003.391Broken. Nonsense.

The model isn't fragile. It doesn't shatter at the first perturbation. There's a wide band—roughly 0.05 to 0.5—where behavior changes smoothly but coherence is maintained. Below that band, the edits are invisible. Above it, the model degrades. The interesting stuff lives right in the transition zone, and it's surprisingly wide.

The existence of this band is not obvious. The model could have been brittle. It isn't. Behavior is a smooth function of weights, and there's room to explore.

Nested Control: The Hierarchy Result

This is where it gets genuinely novel. What happens when you turn two knobs at once?

We fix the Abstraction Dial (Direction 0) at two different settings, then sweep a second direction (Direction 8) within each regime. If the two knobs were independent, the second sweep would do the same thing regardless of the first setting. That's not what happens.

Look at the entropy column. Under concrete mode, sweeping Knob B moves entropy across a range of 0.13 nats (from −0.034 to +0.095). Under poetic mode, the same sweep only covers 0.03 nats.

Knob B doesn't do the same thing in both regimes. The Abstraction Dial gates what Knob B can do. In concrete mode, Knob B has a lot of room to vary the output. In poetic mode, it's constrained. This is nested control—the fine knob's effect depends on the coarse setting. The coarse knob defines a regime, and the fine knob operates within it.

This is the kind of structure you'd expect from a well-designed control system. We didn't design it. It just fell out of random low-rank weight edits.

How It Works

The technical setup is simple. We freeze TinyStories-33M and define 16 rank-1 LoRA basis directions on the attention output projection and MLP output projection of each layer. Each basis direction is a pair of random vectors producing a rank-1 weight edit. A 16-dimensional action vector controls how much of each edit to apply.

action a ∈ R¹&sup6; → Δw = Σ_k a_k · (A_k ⊗ B_k) → w' = w + Δw → generate

We wrap this in a model-based RL loop. An actor (Gaussian policy, trained with GRPO) learns to sample interesting action vectors. A world model (small transformer) learns to predict the behavioral consequences of an action without running the real model. After enough real rollouts, the actor can "dream"—exploring weight space through the world model's predictions.

Training dynamics
Phase 1 (real rollouts):  reward +2.88 → +3.18  |  world model MSE: 2.18 → 0.07
Phase 2 (dreaming):      dream reward: +4.46    |  real validation: +3.11

The reward function doesn't know what a "good" behavioral change looks like. It rewards structure: high KL divergence (the edit did something), low KL variance across prompts (it did the same thing to every prompt), and maintained coherence. The actor learns to find consistent, global behavioral shifts rather than prompt-specific noise.

What This Means

Linear probing showed us that representations are linearly structured. Steering vectors showed us that activations vary linearly. This is the weight-space version of the same story: behavior varies smoothly in weight space, and low-rank edits are enough to access that variation.

But the RL framing adds something new. We're not searching for specific effects. We're doing unsupervised behavioral cartography—discovering what kinds of changes are possible, without knowing what we're looking for. The actor explores, the world model maps the territory, and what falls out are named, interpretable behavioral axes that nobody put there.

This is v1, run on a 33M-parameter toy model. The random basis works, but a trained basis should find sharper axes. The world model works, but it's optimistic (dream reward overshoots real reward by ~30%). The hierarchy is real, but it's faint at this scale.

The question is what happens at 7B parameters, with rank-8 edits instead of rank-1, and a world model that's had time to learn the real landscape. If random directions in a 33M model already have names, what's hiding in the weight space of a model that's 200x larger?

Code: ~700 lines of pure PyTorch + transformers. No RL libraries. The environment, actor, world model, and training loop each fit in a single file.
